The cost of an Ivy League education has once again crossed new thresholds. At Brown University, one of the United States’ most prestigious institutions, the estimated annual cost of pursuing an undergraduate degree in economics for the 2025–26 academic year stands at a staggering $96,284. This amount includes not just tuition but also housing, meals, fees, books, and personal expenses, bringing the four-year total to nearly $385,136.The figures, released by Brown’s admissions and financial aid offices, reflect the full “sticker price” for students without financial aid, making Brown one of the costliest destinations for higher education globally.The cost breakdown for 2025–26Over four years, the cost of an economics degree at Brown is projected to exceed $385,000, excluding additional expenses like travel and health insurance.Here is an estimated annual cost breakdown:ExpenseAnnual Estimate (USD)Tuition$71,700Fees$2,950Housing$10,410Food$8,104Books/Materials$1,300Personal$2,820Miscellaneous$4,120Total$96,284Tuition at Brown crosses $70,000 markOne of the most striking developments is that Brown’s tuition alone has crossed $70,000 for the first time in its history [10]. This milestone is emblematic of a larger trend across the Ivy League and other elite U.S. universities, where annual increases in tuition continue to outpace inflation and average income growth.For families across North America, Europe, Asia, and beyond, this raises difficult questions: is the prestige of an Ivy League degree still worth the price, or are there more sustainable alternatives abroad?Sticker price vs. real priceWhile the published figures are intimidating, Brown emphasises that they do not reflect the “real” cost most students pay. According to the university:Need-based aid: Brown follows a need-blind admissions policy for US applicants and a need-aware system for international students. The institution claims to meet 100% of demonstrated financial need.Scholarships and grants: These can significantly reduce costs, with some students paying a fraction of the sticker price.Work-study opportunities: On-campus jobs provide modest earnings to cover incidental expenses.However, access to aid is uneven. US students typically benefit more from generous packages, while international applicants face tougher competition and stricter eligibility.Global context: How Brown comparesBrown’s $96,000 annual cost is among the highest in the world. For comparison:Other Ivy League universities like Harvard, Yale, and Princeton have similar sticker prices, though their aid packages often bring net costs down.Top UK universities such as Oxford and Cambridge charge international undergraduates in economics roughly £35,000–£40,000 per year ($45,000–$50,000), about half of Brown’s total cost when housing and living expenses are factored in.European universities in countries like Germany, France, and the Netherlands offer competitive economics programs with much lower tuition fees, though living expenses can vary.This contrast highlights the widening gap between US elite institutions and their global peers in terms of affordability.The value questionSupporters of Brown’s pricing model argue that the return on investment justifies the cost. An Ivy League degree, they say, offers unparalleled academic resources, access to top faculty, global networking opportunities, and a career launchpad that often pays off in the long term.Critics, however, counter that skyrocketing tuition risks pricing out talented students, eroding diversity, and fuelling questions about whether US higher education has become a privilege only for the wealthy or the heavily indebted.The bottom lineAt more than $96,000 per year, studying economics at Brown University in 2025–26 underscores both the enduring prestige and the daunting expense of an Ivy League education. While financial aid can ease the burden for many, the full price tag places Brown among the most expensive universities in the world.For families and students worldwide, the decision comes down to weighing the prestige of Ivy League credentials against the very real challenge of affordability in an era of escalating education costs.Ready to navigate global policies?
